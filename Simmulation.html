1. Question¶

¿What are the negative effects on welfare when people make their consumption decisions without full knowledge of their preferences?

2. Motivation¶

Pension funds should be governed properly to ensure benefits from decision making with respect to investment and consumption decision at an older age. This is vulnerable when people base their decisions on wrong parameters. In that sense, we evaluate the welfare costs of suboptimal financial planning over the lifecycle, using the benchmark model of Bovenberg, Koijen, Nijman and Teulings (2007) 2.

In the Netherlands the most common form of pension system is the collective one. But currently few individual elements of choice have been introduced. It has been demonstrated that individual choice may be beneficial for people, if individual preferences are known. However, individual choice can make people worse off if people lacks of knowledge. We show how much the welfare loss is when individual preferences are not known.

This study consists in two cases. The first case investigates suboptimal investments decisions as consequence of decision-making based on the wrong parameter of risk aversion ($\theta$). We check how overestimation or underestimation of risk aversion affects the welfare. The second case examines suboptimal investment decisions that result of decision making based on the wrong parameter of time preference ($\rho$). When time preference is higher than the optimal level, we can observe a consumption preference for the near future as opposed to the distant future and vice versa.

3. Method¶

In this section we are going to examine the effects of a misspecification of the optimal risk aversion parameter, $\theta$, and the optimal time preference parameter, $\rho$. The benchmark model of L.Bovenberg, R.Koijen, T.Nijman and C.Teulings 2 is used and the true values for $\theta$ the risk aversion parameter, and $\rho$ the time preference parameter are equal to $\theta=5$ and $\rho=0.02$. The effect on consumption and financial wealth are investigated and compared to the true values.

    e=start_age+(i*deltat)
    age.append(e)

def Funcion_Y(i): #Labour earnings
    if age[i-1]<start_age+T:
        return 1
    else:
        return 0
    
def Funcion_H(i): #Human Capital
        e=(1/r)*(1-math.exp(-r*max(0,(start_age+T-age[i-1]))))
        return e

def Funcion_A(theta, rho):
    w=((1-theta)*r-rho)/theta+0.5*((1-theta)/theta**2)*lambda_v**2
    return w

def Funcion_g(i, theta, rho):
    e=(1/Funcion_A(theta, rho))*(math.exp(Funcion_A(theta, rho)*(D-(age[i-1]-start_age)))-1)
    return e

#Adding extra g, needed for certainty equivalence
def Funcion_g_ce(theta, rho):
    e=(1/Funcion_A(theta, rho))*(math.exp(Funcion_A(theta, rho)*D)-1)
    return e

            Matrix=np.ones(50000)
            MatrixF=Matrix*e
            w=sum(MatrixF)/len(MatrixF)
            return w

def Funcion_C(n,theta,rho): #Consumption
    for n in range(1,60,1):
        e=(Funcion_F(n,theta,rho)+Funcion_H(n))*Funcion_g(n,theta,rho)**(-1)
        return e 
    
def Funcion_fstar_times_F(n,theta,rho):
    for n in range(1,60,1):
        e=(1/theta)*(lambda_v/sigma)*(Funcion_F(n,theta,rho)+Funcion_H(n))
        return e
    
def Funcion_fstar(n,theta,rho): #Portfolio choice (percentage of wealth invested in risky assets)
    if n==1:
        return float('nan')
    else:
        for n in range(2,60,1):
            e=Funcion_fstar_times_F(n,theta,rho)/Funcion_F(n,theta,rho)
            return e

4. Results¶

Suboptimal Investment Decision that Result if Decision Making is based upon the Wrong Parameter of Relative Risk Aversion¶

Effect on consumption¶

If a decision is made based on another value of the risk aversion we get a sub-optimal outcome. The effect on median consumption can be found in Figure 1.

The higher the value of $\theta$, the more risk averse the individual. When analyzing the effect of a change in the risk aversion parameter in consumption, we ought to remember that the less risk averse the person is, the more risky choices of investment she will make, which will lead to higher profits on average. This result can be seen in Figure 2, the lower the risk aversion the higher the part in risky assets. This is why we can see that as we consider lower values for $\theta$, the median value of consumption rises. Also, because less averse people will make riskier and more profitable choices through time, the expected accumulated return will be higher the longer time we consider. So, for every value of $\theta$, the trend is growing upwards, but for low values of $\theta$ the growth rate of this trend is higher. It is also important to note that the variation of outcomes do increase as the risk aversion parameter decreases.

If the average person in the pension fund has a lower risk aversion then the individual has (i.e. $\theta < 5$), the fund will invest more risky than the individual with $\theta = 5$ expects. Then we expect that the consumption will be lower than the average retiree what causes a welfare loss for the individual. The size of the welfare loss will be discussed in section 5.

Effect on financial wealth¶

We furthermore look at the effect on the mean financial wealth, pictured in Figure 3. Here, we see that lower values of $\theta$ are linked to higher financial wealth at retirement age. This, because a less risk averse person is more likely to make riskier investment choices, which will yield a higher profit and higher accumulation of financial wealth. Suboptimal Investment Decision that Result if Decision Making is based upon the Wrong Parameter of Time Preference¶

In the previous section we examined the effect of decision making based upon the wrong parameter of risk aversion, in addition we examined the effect of a wrong parameter of time preference ($\rho$). The same aspects are evaluated as in the previous section.

Effect on consumption¶

If a decision is made based on another value of the time preference, we get a suboptimal outcome. The effect on consumption can be found in Figure 4.

The median consumption levels corresponding with the varying values for time preference parameter $\rho$, can be found in Figure 4. Individuals with a higher time preference than the actual rate of time preference, which is $\rho=0.02$, tend to prioritize the consumption of their near future over the consumption of their distant future. This is confirmed by Figure 4, as the investment strategies with a higher time preference have a higher rate of consumption during their early stage of life, but a relatively lower consumption when they are old. At parameter $\rho=.0.5$, time preference is so high it results in a downward slope, but at a decreasing rate over time. The individual severely prefers to increase his current consumption at the cost of his future consumption. On the other hand, individuals with a lower time preference $\rho$ tend to place relatively more weight on their old-age consumption compared to their young-age consumption, which is in line with the results represented in Figure 4.

Effect on financial wealth¶

We furthermore look at the effect on the financial wealth. A lower value of $\rho$ will mean that individuals will value future consumption more, relative to current consumption. In this section, we will look at the following: The lower the value of $\rho$, the more the person will consume in the future in relation to what she will consume in the present, and also if the time preference is smaller than the benchmark the less she will consume now. In order to pay for this consumption, the person with a higher value of $\rho$ needs to have higher financial wealth at the early stages of life. If a person does not value future consumption as much, then she will not need a very high financial wealth at retirement age.

It is shown in Figure 5 that the financial wealth reaches it’s highest value when $\rho=0.01$, and it’s lowest when $\rho=0.05$.

If investments of pension funds where to be made based on a wrong estimation of $\rho$, the accumulation of assets at retirement age would be different than what retirees prefer. I.e., a higher value of $\rho$ would mean that people reach their retirement age with less assets than desired, and a lower value of $\rho$ would result in an accumulation higher than desired.

5. Welfare Loss due to Wrong Parameter Values¶

In the current political debate about pensions in the Netherlands choice is an important topic. If choice is offered, participants can gain if their optimal preference parameters are different from the average participant. However the literature shows that participants don’t know their own preferences and that the architectural design of choice can influence the participants. Benartzi and Thaler 1 have studied the behavior regarding retirement investments of participants in defined contribution pension schemes. They have found that participants allocate their retirement savings dependent on the portfolio choices offered to them. If this results in a sub-optimal outcome, it may indicate welfare loss on individual level. In order to study the effect of wrong parameter specification, certainty equivalents are computed and compared to the optimal results of the benchmark model by Bovenberg et al. 2. The results are shown in terms of welfare loss.

$$E_t \left( \int_{t}^{D} \frac{e^{-\rho \left( s-t \right)}}{1-\theta} C_s^{1-\theta*}ds \right)=\int_{t}^{D} \frac{e^{-\rho \left( s-t \right)}}{1-\theta} ce_s^{1-\theta}ds$$

The certainty equivalent is computed using the following equation contained in the appendix (A5) of Bovenberg et al. 2.

We computed the certainty equivalents for a consumption-investment policy. The certainty equivalent consumption level $ce$ follows from equation (A27) contained in the appendix of Bovenberg et al. Here $W_0$ is the wealth of the investor at period $0$. For further explanation on parameter $g$ we refer to the appendix of the paper of Bovenberg et al 2.

$$ce=\left(W_0^{1-\theta} g\left(D\right)^\theta \frac{\rho}{1-e^{\rho D}}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\theta}}$$

We have computed the certainty equivalent for several parameters including the true ones, we can calculate the welfare loss. The welfare loss is equal to:

$$\frac{ce^{true}-ce}{ce}$$

In the last equation, $ce^{true}$ is the certainty equivalent based on the true values of the parameters which are: $\rho=0.02$ and $\theta=5$.
